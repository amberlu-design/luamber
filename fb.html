Facebook Messenger


Facebook Messenger, although a great messaging app, still has room for iterations and additional features. For this case study, I dive into researching why and how Facebook Messenger could be improved and designing the best solution to make this product even more convenient for the users.


Scope

UX Research & Design


Duration

2 Weeks









Facebook Messenger and its Problems


I love Facebook Messenger, it's the only messaging app I use because of it's connection to the main Facebook app.

I constantly catch myself scrolling through endless messages in a chat, trying to find the one specific message. I realized that not only I had this problem but I often see people around me doing so as well.

You might say: "just use the search in chat feature!" Yeah...but what if you are looking for the address for your friend's place's address? Or a plan made a few weeks ago (especially when you can't get an instant response)? In these scenarios, you don't remember the specific phrase used in the message, so it would be almost impossible to find.







User Research


For my user research, I interviewed and observed 5 participants. Goals: I wanted to find out their usage behaviors and the struggles they have when navigating through this app.


Key Findings

1. Can't easily find messages sent previously

"search chat" doesn't work because sometimes users can't remember the specific word used in the chat, doesn't work when trying to find photos either


2. Sometimes miss messages sent by people who they talk to the most often


3. Forget if a group chat already exists, end up creating a duplicate

Entry Point Design Sketches

How can the pinned messages entry point be displayed for easy access and not increase cognitive load? How many steps should the users take to get to the entry point?



Design A isn't the best solution because 1) it's not easily accessable, 2) by displaying all pinned messages in the chat setting, it would feel unorganized because content of different categories are displayed on the same information hierarchy.

Design B requires users to take 2 steps to access the archive. This makes the message feel more of high importance, like notes, instead of just marking them as important. This would trigger users to pin as many messages as they want since there's even a separate archive or them and don't suggest casualness or instantness. Eventually, users might pin too many messages that they would have to search within the pinned messages, which doesn't solve our initial problem.

Design D makes the pinned messages feel more casual and instant but takes up more cognitive load than necessary. Some messages don't have to be displayed and seen by users at all times, they just need to be easily accessable when needed. Also, more than one message may be pinned meaning that the pinned message block would have to somehow rotate or have a pull down to show other messages.

Design C is the best solution. The messages are hidden initially and are shown when the icon is clicked on, provides instant access messages, a popup-like block would allow the users to think twice before pinning a message for a curated selection of messages.





Pinned Messages wireframes

How can the pinned messages stand out without getting in the way of current chatroom designs? How should the relationship between pinned messages and entry point be shown?



I conducted usability testing to see which design best solves the problem.

Design A has a thickened box outline and a background color change. This is a clean way for the pinned message to standout, however, Messenger allows users to change the theme color of the chatroom. The solution of having a box outline and color change would clash with this feature.

Design B is the better solution here. A pin icon shown on the message boxes' corners is a slick way to make it stand out and also clearly show a relationship between the messages and the pin icon of the entry point.
